Te Wairere Waterfall

Te Wairere Waterfall (meaning "flying waters" in Māori) is one of Kerikeri's most picturesque natural attractions, nestled in lush native bush just a short drive from the town center. This stunning 15-meter waterfall cascades gracefully into a serene pool below, surrounded by native ferns and trees that create a tranquil, magical atmosphere.

The waterfall flows year-round but is particularly impressive after rainfall when the water volume increases dramatically. Located just a short drive from Kerikeri, this natural wonder offers visitors a chance to experience the peaceful beauty of Northland's native bush and waterways.

The name "Te Wairere" refers to the way the water appears to "fly" as it cascades down the rock face. The area surrounding the waterfall has cultural significance to local Māori, who have traditional connections to this land and its waterways dating back many generations.

Quick Facts

Height:
Approximately 15 meters
Type:
Single drop waterfall
Walking Time:
15-20 minutes from car park
Swimming:
Not recommended (cold water, potential hazards)
Difficulty:
Moderate - Some steep sections
Best Time:
After rainfall for maximum water flow
Facilities:
Car park only (no toilets)

Visitor Tips

  • Wear sturdy footwear with good grip, especially after rain when the track can be slippery.
  • Bring insect repellent during summer months to ward off mosquitoes.
  • The best time to visit is after rainfall when the waterfall is at its most impressive.
  • Morning light provides the best illumination for photos.
  • Carry water and snacks if planning to spend time at the falls.
  • Stay on marked tracks to protect the native vegetation.
  • The rocks around the waterfall can be extremely slippery - take care when approaching the water.
  • Swimming is not recommended due to cold water temperatures and potential underwater hazards.
  • There is limited mobile phone reception in the area.

Walking Tracks

Te Wairere Falls is accessible via a moderate grade walking track:

Main Track:
15-20 minute walk from the car park through native bush.
Upper Viewing Platform:
Additional 5-minute climb from the main viewing area.
Difficulty:
Moderate - Some steep sections and potentially slippery surfaces.

The track takes you through beautiful native bush with information boards along the way providing insights into the local flora and fauna. Multiple viewing platforms offer different perspectives of the falls.

Cultural Significance

Te Wairere Falls has cultural importance to the local Māori. The name "Te Wairere" refers to the way the water appears to "fly" as it cascades down the rock face.

The area surrounding the waterfall has been significant to local iwi (tribes) for generations, with the waterways providing sustenance and the surrounding bush offering medicinal plants and materials for traditional crafts.

The falls and surrounding area are considered a taonga (treasure) by local Māori, representing the connection between the people and the natural environment.

Today, the falls are protected as part of a scenic reserve, ensuring this natural and cultural treasure is preserved for future generations to enjoy.
